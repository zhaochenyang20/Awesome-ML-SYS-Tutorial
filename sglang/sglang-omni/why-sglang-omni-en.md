Those who know me are aware that I do not come from a systems background — in fact, for a long time I had an instinctive fear of systems work. I once wrote the following in a post shared with friends:

My heartfelt thanks to Graham and Sherry, who advised me that summer, and to Vijay, the patient PhD who walked me through everything. They were the first to show me the beauty of systems.

> But truly recognizing the power of systems had to wait until after I graduated. Honestly, even after I had seen the beauty of systems, I did not put much more heart into systems courses during my undergrad. To this day I still feel ashamed admitting that most of my hardcore systems courses — Computer Organization, Computer Networks, Operating Systems — were all completed in my senior year, once my GPA no longer mattered. Years later, having spent every day dealing with these fundamentals, I realize I never properly learned them. I still remember that there was an elective by Professor Mingxing Zhang I could have taken before graduation, but I simply was not in a state to handle it. It is precisely this less-than-glorious undergraduate chapter that has made me carry a careful self-awareness ever since stepping into systems research. I know clearly that my fundamentals have gaps — almost every step forward requires me to pay back something I owed in the past. And so it goes — from SGLang RL to SGLang Omni, the problems we work on appear to have changed entirely. Before, we dealt with GPU memory offload and upload, and studied how to efficiently transfer parameters between training and inference engines. Now, with Omni, we think about fusion between stages, and how to cover the entire decoding process with a single unified [CUDA Graph](../../torch/cuda-graph/readme-2-en.md). This is a fascinating shift — I am almost working on something completely unrelated to before, and whenever I mention to friends that I am now doing SGLang Omni, people sometimes look puzzled and ask why I have switched to something so different.

My thinking on this: on the one hand, I have long shared a perspective from Junrong, an early member of the SGLang RL group — "Our RL system optimizations are often bottlenecked by our understanding of the inference system itself." So going deeper into inference is, in fact, of great benefit to the RL work I love. On the other hand, Omni models themselves have RL needs — TTS and the Thinker in Qwen3 Omni both have clear reward targets.

More importantly, the core tasks and methods of ML systems are highly unified. Parameter offload-and-refit in RL and stage fusion in Omni look unrelated, but both are about designing reasonable structures for specific computational topologies, and then pushing communication and scheduling optimizations to the extreme. Although the models SGLang Omni faces differ greatly from LLMs, the core computational characteristics of the decoding phase remain deeply shared, and the optimization ideas often converge from different paths. In my view, an ML systems researcher has only one goal: study the computational characteristics of a given computation, and design an efficient and robust system tailored to those characteristics.

This article is therefore both a systematic summary of the current technical framework and stage-of-work of the SGLang Omni team, and our serious attempt to answer a few fundamental questions:

1. What kind of computation are we trying to optimize?
2. What computational characteristics does this computation have?
3. What kind of system have we designed for it?
4. Where do we hope SGLang Omni will go next?

Fortunately, this time I am not exploring alone either. Starting from the SGLang RL community, we have gathered more and more companions. Even though some may not stay at the open-source frontline forever — life and work change — the strong cohesion of the community and the passion for filling every GPU remains. This time I have once again been lucky to meet equally outstanding companions. Together we re-examine the inference process of Omni models, and design an inference framework rooted in computational characteristics. SGLang Omni is still under active development, and we are always welcoming friends who share our aesthetic obsession with ML systems to join us.

## What Kind of Computation Are We Optimizing?

Back in April last year, when the Qwen team first released Qwen 2.5 Omni, we already tried to support Qwen 2.5 Omni in SGLang main. From a modality standpoint, the inputs of Qwen 2.5 VL and Qwen 2.5 Omni are quite similar — the Thinker of Qwen 2.5 Omni can be understood as Qwen 2.5 VL with an additional audio input. Logically, setting aside the specific technical route of the audio encoder, the Thinker of Qwen 2.5 Omni can be abstracted as a stronger, more multi-modal Qwen 2.5 VL. Unfortunately, the technical route of Omni models is not just this simple — and here we hit another question: the definition of an Omni model is itself not unified.


Under some model teams' definitions, an Omni Model counts as a VLM that supports audio input — examples include [MiMo Omni](https://mimo.xiaomi.com/mimo-v2-omni) and [Nemotron Omni](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24954). Under the Qwen Omni team's definition, however, an Omni Model not only supports audio input but also audio output. An example I often bring up is GPT-4o and Google's Gemini App. Imagine that you are grocery shopping and open Gemini App in camera mode — the model takes in video from the camera while simultaneously accepting your voice input. On the output side, you see text appearing on screen alongside corresponding audio output. In terms of input/output modalities, the Omni line represented by Qwen supports inputs of "voice + text + video + image," and outputs of "text + voice." Such an Omni definition is also fairly common — for example, Grok APP on Tesla and the Doubao mobile APP. The most comprehensive Omni models go even further: [Ming Omni](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.09344) from the Ant team achieves fully omni-modal input and output. To support both text and image output, it employs both autoregressive decoding and denoising/diffusion on the output side. Beyond that, the LLaDA team — long-standing collaborators with SGLang — has released [LLaDA Uni](https://github.com/inclusionAI/LLaDA2.0-Uni), which, like Ming Omni, offers astonishing omni-modal capabilities.

Among such varied Omni definitions, I personally have no preference — every model team has put in enormous effort, and every definition stands in its own algorithmic context. Only, for those of us working on inference systems, slicing by input/output modality is not a particularly reasonable angle — modality tells us what the outward packaging of a model looks like, but does not directly reveal its computation. As I mentioned, ML systems research cares about computation itself, and Auto Regressive Decoding is such a unified and elegant modeling paradigm. Slicing by modality loses some of that beauty.

So let us take a different angle: what kind of model is SGLang Omni designed, from day one, to serve well? Our criterion is not modality, but whether the model's decoding process is multi-stage. What we are dividing here is computation, not model quality — every model mentioned below is backed by a team's solid and impressive work. We are merely choosing the computational dimension and using multi-stage decoding as the classification axis.

We take Qwen3-Omni as an example to explain the general flow of multi-stage decoding (the detailed process can be found in [my earlier blog post](../../transformers/omni/readme-en.md)). Qwen3-Omni generates outputs containing both text and speech, not via one decode loop running autoregressively from start to finish, but through an alternation of several heterogeneous decode stages: Thinker autoregressively generates text; Talker autoregressively generates the 0-th codec token for each timestep; MTP then completes the remaining codec tokens for that timestep — together these decode stages form an asynchronous pipeline. In the SGLang Omni philosophy, the key thing about Qwen3-Omni is not multi-modality, but that end-to-end decoding has been split into several stages with distinct computational characters. This is multi-stage decoding.

Interestingly, multi-stage decoding is not exclusive to Omni models — many TTS models adopt a multi-stage decoding process as well. Take FishAudio's S2 Pro: it uses a two-stage pipeline called Dual-AR to generate speech — an approximately 4B Slow AR generates semantic tokens frame by frame along the time axis, and an approximately 400M Fast AR completes the acoustic codec tokens within each frame. The two stages are nested serially. Such models have no mission to understand multi-modal inputs, but on the decoding side, their multi-stage structure is in spirit very close to Qwen3-Omni.

Viewed from the angle of "is decoding multi-stage or not", models from various teams naturally fall on two sides, each with its most fitting inference pattern.

| Category | Representative Models | Decoding Pattern | Within SGLang Omni's Design Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single-stage (AR) | MiMo Omni, Nemotron Omni, Qwen ASR, MiMo ASR | A single standard LLM/VLM decode loop | No — SGLang main already optimizes this to the extreme |
| Single-stage (diffusion) | Wan, Qwen-Image | A single denoising loop | No — [SGLang Diffusion](../diffusion-llm/readme-en.md) already supports it |
| Multi-stage | Qwen3-Omni, Fish S2 Pro, Qwen3-TTS, Voxtral, Higgs, Ming Omni, LLaDA Uni | Multiple heterogeneous decode stages in serial / parallel | Yes |

On one side is single-stage decoding. Models like MiMo Omni and Nemotron Omni can understand rich inputs — audio, video, images — and produce text outputs. Their decoding is a standard LLM / VLM decode loop. Qwen ASR and MiMo ASR take audio in and produce text transcripts, similarly. These are very powerful models, and their decoding happens to be exactly what SGLang main is best at — SGLang itself can already push their inference to the extreme, with no additional need for a multi-stage pipeline layer.

The other side is multi-stage decoding — the missing piece SGLang Omni wants to fill. Qwen3-Omni, many TTS models (S2 Pro, Qwen3-TTS, Voxtral, Higgs, etc.), and fully omni-modal output models like Ming Omni and LLaDA Uni — all of them have decoding split into multiple heterogeneous stages. These models also embody admirable work from many teams, and the multi-stage computation they share is exactly what inspired our design of SGLang Omni.

It is no coincidence that diffusion models can also be classified clearly this way. Excellent open-source image generators like Wan and Qwen-Image have decoding consisting of a single denoising loop, with no second decode stage to relay — they are single-stage. [SGLang Diffusion](../diffusion-llm/readme-en.md) already has a leading inference solution dedicated to diffusion, so these models are not within SGLang Omni's design targets, even though "image output" sounds very Omni. Conversely, Ming Omni also uses diffusion, but its diffusion block is just one stage within its multi-stage pipeline (an AR backbone first generates, then routes into the audio decoder or the diffusion image decoder). So Ming Omni is multi-stage, and is a target of SGLang Omni. Under SGLang Omni's design principle of slicing by computation, what determines a model's category is not whether it uses diffusion, but whether its decoding is multi-stage. The same image generator — as a pure image model it is everything, but inside Ming Omni it is just one stage. The surrounding computation is entirely different. We are proud of this — SGLang Omni's design target is both unified and precise.

One more interesting rule of thumb: models with audio output tend to fall within SGLang Omni's supported scope. Because the mainstream technical route for speech generation almost always splits decoding into an AR backbone plus codec completion — multiple stages — audio output and multi-stage are strongly correlated. But I always insist that computation is the essential starting point for ML systems design. ML systems researchers and algorithm researchers may have different aesthetic pursuits.

## What Computational Characteristics Do Multi-Stage Decoding Models Have?

The previous section established the kind of model SGLang Omni truly wants to serve — models whose decoding is split into multiple heterogeneous stages. A natural question is: what do these models actually share computationally? And how will these shared characteristics shape our trade-offs when building the inference system?

Let us start from a concrete example — the difference between Qwen 2.5 VL and Qwen 2.5 Omni Thinker-Talker. For Thinker, the picture is relatively clear and can be defined as a VLM with an extra audio encoder. But once Talker enters the picture, the computational processes of Thinker and Talker diverge significantly — in memory access patterns, scheduling cadence, and latency sensitivity.

Thinker is a standard autoregressive decode loop. Its bottleneck lies in attention and KV cache management, and the goal is to raise both TPOT and throughput. SGLang main has accumulated a mature toolkit for this — prefill/decode disaggregation, chunked prefill, continuous batching, and so on. Talker, however, is different. Talker itself is an autoregressive backbone that generates the 0-th codec token of the current timestep each step. Immediately after, MTP conditions on Talker's first codec token, completes the remaining codec tokens for that timestep in parallel, and writes the resulting embeddings back to Talker as the input condition for the next step. The feedback loop is not between Thinker and Talker, but between Talker and MTP. MTP's outputs are written back into Talker's buffer for Talker to consume in the next step. This causes Talker's per-step computation to no longer be dominated by large GEMMs — instead, it alternates between a lightweight backbone forward and the multi-head completion plus embedding write-back of MTP. Moreover, Talker's latency requirement is more stringent than Thinker's — how quickly the user hears the first syllable depends on how fast Talker completes the first timestep's backbone + MTP, but each step's compute is far less than Thinker's, and GPU utilization is naturally low. We can therefore understand Thinker's prefill as compute-bound, and Thinker's decode as memory-bound. These two bounds are widely studied in ML systems: during prefill, GPU compute is the bottleneck — we want to saturate all matmuls; during decode, memory bandwidth is the bottleneck — every read of a long key/value history from KV cache costs far more than running a new matmul, so the core of decode optimization lies in KV cache management and compressing memory access patterns.

Talker and MTP are more complex and non-standard. Talker's backbone is autoregressive and looks like a small decode loop, but each step does not read a long-sequence KV cache. Its inputs are just the Thinker embedding of the current step and the codec embedding fed back by MTP. Attention is extremely light, and memory bandwidth is far from the bottleneck — it is not memory-bound. MTP's completion resembles a tiny prefill, but each call processes only a handful of codec tokens. The compute is too small to even approach the compute ceiling — it is not compute-bound. When the two are chained together, the real characteristic is latency sensitivity, extremely tight inter-step feedback dependency, and very light per-step operations. Kernel launch overhead and synchronization overhead instead become the dominant concerns.

### Heterogeneous Computation Paradigms

Lay these three computation paradigms side by side, and the first shared characteristic of multi-stage decoding becomes obvious: the compute intensity, memory access pattern, and latency tolerance differ widely across stages. If we implemented this directly inside the single Scheduler in the SGLang main repo, three different paradigms — compute-bound, memory-bound, and latency-sensitive-yet-neither — would be force-stitched onto a single end-to-end pipeline. Thinker's throughput would be disrupted by Talker's fine-grained operations, and Talker's latency would be dragged down by Thinker's large-batch prefill. Heterogeneity makes decoupling not an option but a necessity — Thinker and Talker can only be scheduled asynchronously by two separate SGLang Schedulers.

### Divergent Dependency Patterns

Heterogeneity brings more than a need for scheduling decoupling — how data flows between stages also has to be handled case by case. The second shared characteristic is that the dependency patterns between stages have diverged. LLM inference has a unidirectional producer-consumer relationship: prefill produces KV cache, decode consumes it. But in the multi-stage scenario there are at least two distinct data dependency patterns. Thinker and Talker are asynchronously decoupled — tokens and hidden states produced by Thinker enter a shared buffer, and Talker consumes at its own pace. The two maintain independent decode loops and do not need to march in lockstep. Talker and MTP, on the other hand, are synchronously tightly coupled — every time Talker produces a 0-th codec token, MTP must launch its completion immediately and write back the embedding, and Talker's next step strictly depends on that write-back. These two dependency patterns demand different communication mechanisms — the former needs a low-overhead streaming buffer with some slack, while the latter requires ultra-low single-step latency and minimal scheduling overhead.

### Memory Contention

If scheduling and communication are about "motion," then memory is about "occupation." The third computational characteristic is memory management. Single-Scheduler model inference is very clear: weights take up part of the GPU, the rest goes to KV cache (details in [my earlier blog post](../kvcache-code-walk-through/mem-fraction-static-en.md)). But in the multi-stage scenario, the weights of both Thinker and Talker must reside in memory; Thinker needs its own prefix cache; various encoders need memory reserved for long sequences; Talker needs a feedback buffer between itself and MTP; Talker also needs its own KV cache… The problem is not just that more players are sharing the cake — different stages have different load orders, different offload strategies, and the meaning of "remaining available memory" varies step by step. The memory fraction semantics of a single SGLang scheduler need to be redefined under multi-stage, and we have to build a full cross-stage memory allocation mechanism.

Putting these computational characteristics together, what multi-stage decoding demands from an inference framework gradually becomes clear. The scheduling and execution of heterogeneous stages need to be elegantly decoupled; we need an inter-stage streaming communication mechanism with controlled overhead; and memory and compute resources need to be carefully budgeted and isolated. These demands are deeply invasive to SGLang itself — but once we wrap each stage as one SGLang Scheduler, the picture clears up. Starting from these computational characteristics, we need to redesign a full SGLang Omni architecture.

## What Kind of System Have We Designed?

The previous section ended with three concrete demands — heterogeneous stages need elegant scheduling decoupling, cross-stage communication needs controlled overhead, and cross-stage memory needs careful budgeting. This section lands each of them in turn, letting every stage compute in the way it does best, minimizing communication overhead, and budgeting and isolating memory and compute resources at fine granularity.

### Scheduling Decoupling

As described earlier, on the scheduling side, we need to prevent the three paradigms — compute-bound, memory-bound, and latency-sensitive lightweight serial execution — from dragging one another down inside a complex loop. The approach is now clear: reuse SGLang Scheduler's existing scheduling design for a single stage, but wrap each stage into its own independent Scheduler with its own scheduling loop.

For AR stages like Thinker, we directly reuse the mature scheduling capability of SGLang Scheduler — OmniScheduler keeps all the key abilities including continuous batching, mixed prefill/decode scheduling, KV cache management, tree cache, overlap scheduling, while dropping modules currently unneeded in the Omni scenario such as tokenizer, grammar, and speculative decoding. For stages that need no scheduling such as preprocessing and encoders, SimpleScheduler is just a straightforward `get → forward → put` loop. For streaming stages like vocoder, Code2WavScheduler maintains per-request accumulated state, decodes by window, and emits audio frames.

The scheduling of Talker is the most interesting problem. Talker is itself an AR model, generating codec tokens frame by frame along the time axis, with its own paged KV cache management needs. So it also runs atop OmniScheduler, maintaining an independent scheduling loop alongside Thinker, with the two asynchronously decoupled through relay. But Talker and MTP are synchronously coupled — every time Talker produces a 0-th codec token, MTP must immediately complete the remaining tokens of the current timestep and write back the embedding; Talker's next step strictly depends on that write-back. If we split Talker and MTP into two Stages with a relay and a ZMQ signal in between, per-step latency would balloon. Instead, we place Talker and MTP within the same Stage — MTP's completion and feedback write-back are all encapsulated inside a single forward call of FeedbackARModelRunner. As far as the upper-layer Coordinator is concerned, one timestep of Talker + MTP is just one lightweight decode step — it is entirely unaware of MTP's existence. To stress this clearly: merging them into the same Stage only changes the ordering of kernels and the boundary of the CUDA Graph. Talker's own paged KV cache, MTP's own weights and multi-head completion logic — none of that is changed. They remain two complete models, simply sharing the same forward call. The tight feedback loop between Talker and MTP is confined inside the Stage, with no cross-scheduler overhead, and kernel launches and synchronization can be flattened by a piecewise CUDA Graph. This is the elegant solution for latency-sensitive low-compute serial tasks.

Finally, all Schedulers follow the same inbox/outbox protocol externally — a Stage does not need to know whether a complex AR scheduler or a tens-of-lines simple loop runs behind it. Unified interface, varied implementations — different computation paradigms each get what they need, and a clean extension path is left open for more Stage types in the future.

### Layered Communication

To enable efficient communication between stages, we decompose the communication process into a control plane and a data plane. Control messages — such as "new request arrived", "upstream chunk written", "request aborted" — go through ZMQ: lightweight, mature, and hard to get wrong. The data plane carries the actual large tensors via relay — using shared memory or CUDA IPC for zero-copy between same-machine GPUs, and NCCL or RDMA across nodes.

The layered communication strategy maps directly onto the two dependency patterns analyzed earlier. For the asynchronous decoupling between Thinker and Talker, Thinker writes tokens and hidden states into the relay and sends a DataReady signal; Talker consumes at its own pace; the two maintain independent decode loops and do not need to march in lockstep. For the synchronously tightly-coupled Talker-MTP pair, MTP must wait for Talker to produce the 0-th token, and Talker's next step must wait for MTP to write back. This internal feedback loop is enclosed inside the ModelRunner of the same Stage, requiring no inter-stage communication and avoiding extra scheduling overhead.

Finally, above all the stages, the Coordinator handles high-level orchestration — routing new requests to the entry Stage, collecting outputs from terminal Stages, and merging results when multiple terminals produce simultaneously. It does not specialize for model details; it serves only the pipeline topology.

### Memory Isolation

The multi-stage decoding scenario differs noticeably from single-stage decoding managed by a single Scheduler in terms of memory configuration. We upgrade memory allocation from "a single global ratio per stage" to "a cross-stage budget mechanism." Each stage declares its own `total_gpu_memory_fraction` in its configuration; at startup the system sums per-GPU and validates — exceeding the card capacity gets rejected outright, otherwise AR stages get the preconfigured KV cache space allocated as much as possible. So Thinker and Talker on the same resource group each have their own memory budgets, and an encoder's peak activation is also capped within its declared quota — leaving ample memory for long sequences.

In memory management, encoders are the most easily underestimated risk in Omni models. Qwen3-Omni's vision encoder and audio encoder together weigh only about 2.5 GB — not much. The real pressure comes from activations: long audio or video inputs can push encoder activation peaks well beyond what a single card can hold — a one-minute video can easily exceed 30 GB. Encoders are therefore not auxiliary modules; they are first-class citizens that must be taken seriously in the memory budget. Fortunately, SGLang Omni's architecture handles this very naturally. Just like other stages, encoders declare `tp_size` and GPU placement in their `StageConfig`, while `MultiProcessRunner` unifies process management, NCCL port allocation, and inter-rank synchronization. The ability to TP-shard peak activations is not specially configured for encoders — it applies to all stages by design. The clean reuse of the TP mechanism inside each stage is exactly what reflects SGLang Omni's flexibility in stage abstraction and placement.

## What Do We Hope SGLang Omni Becomes?

By this point, the overall design of the SGLang Omni project speaks for itself. We are also proud — the current SGLang Omni has already started to look the way our team finds aesthetically right, and it is growing vigorously. We believe in the vitality of the SGLang Omni architecture — from concrete computations like Qwen3-Omni's Thinker-Talker asynchronous pipeline, Fish S2 Pro's serially-nested Dual-AR, and Ming Omni's omni-modal output, we have step by step abstracted what is common, with clean boundaries and flexible extension.

Of course, we are far from perfect. Cross-node multi-stage pipelines, more complete diffusion stage support, and end-to-end RL training integration are all underway. But with clean stage abstraction, a unified scheduler interface, layered communication, and cross-stage memory budgeting, these features should fall into place with calm and grace. We look forward to the next phase of SGLang Omni — feature-complete and friendly to the model ecosystem. For the next generation of multi-stage models, there should be no need to build a pipeline from scratch and scatter if-else branches across a dozen files. Elegantly partition the model into scheduling segments, plug in our callback hooks, declare a topology, and leave the rest — scheduling, communication, memory management — to the framework. We hope this will be done, and we believe it can be done.

Naturally, SGLang Omni is an open community project. Like SGLang main, it lives in the PRs and discussions of every contributor. If you read this article, find what we are doing interesting and meaningful, and happen to be thinking about how multi-stage inference frameworks should be designed — and you want to join a group of people in completing a truly industrial-grade engineering training together — we very much welcome you to join. Whether you come from a systems background or arrive halfway, whether you specialize in kernel optimization or scheduling logic, we look forward to your joining us.
